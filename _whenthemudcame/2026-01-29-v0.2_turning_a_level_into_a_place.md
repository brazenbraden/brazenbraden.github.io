This update wasn’t about adding new mechanics or expanding the core gameplay loop. Instead, it was about something more subtle, but just as important: **how the world feels to be in**.

As the project has grown, it became increasingly obvious that some parts of the experience were doing the right things mechanically, but not *communicating* those things well to the player. Invisible walls felt arbitrary. The surrounding environment dropped off too sharply. Some areas felt visually unfinished once they were fully cleaned.

This build is a deliberate polish pass over those issues.

Below is a breakdown of what changed, and more importantly, *why*.

## Expanding the World Beyond the Play Space

One of the biggest immersion killers in first-person games is the sense that the world simply stops existing beyond a certain point. Even if the playable area is well designed, hard edges and empty backdrops subconsciously remind the player that they’re inside a box.

To address that, I expanded the surrounding environment significantly.

The playable area itself hasn’t changed, but what exists *around* it has. The addition of distant terrain, a visible highway shoulder lined with trees, and a volcanic landmark on the horizon all work together to suggest a larger world beyond the immediate play space.

You’re not just cleaning a level — you’re cleaning *a place*.

## Making Boundaries Feel Intentional

Invisible walls are sometimes unavoidable, especially in tightly scoped projects. The problem isn’t their existence; it’s when they feel unjustified.

Previously, some boundaries simply stopped the player without any strong visual explanation. That’s now been addressed by introducing fences, barriers, and other physical cues that make those limits feel deliberate rather than arbitrary.

The goal here wasn’t to hide constraints, but to **align the rules of the game with the logic of the world**. When the player understands *why* they can’t go somewhere, friction disappears almost entirely.

---

## Extending the Road Beyond the Level

As part of expanding the world beyond the cleanable area, the road now continues out into the distance rather than ending abruptly at the edge of the map. This helps visually connect the playable space to the wider environment and reinforces the idea that the level exists within a larger, continuous world.

To support that illusion, greenery generation was made more selective so that road and roadside areas remain visually readable as infrastructure rather than landscape. Nothing was broken in the previous build — this change simply ensures that the extended environment feels intentional and grounded rather than decorative.

---

## Lighting, Atmosphere, and Fog

A lot of this update focused on atmosphere rather than obvious “features”.

Additional light sources were introduced to better shape the environment and guide the eye, especially during low-contrast moments. Combined with performance improvements, this allowed for a more confident lighting pass without negatively impacting frame rate.

The fog was also adjusted so that it moves across the ground rather than sitting statically. This adds a subtle sense of motion and depth, helping the world feel alive even when the player is standing still.

Individually, these changes are easy to miss. Together, they do a lot of heavy lifting.

## Cleaning Values and Player Friction

A few assets were adjusted after observing how they behaved during normal play.

Some surfaces required disproportionately precise cleaning, which made them feel fiddly rather than satisfying. These values have been tweaked so that difficulty comes from *coverage and positioning*, not from fighting the system.

The intent is always to make cleaning feel rewarding, not punitive.

---

## Cleaned Zones Should Feel Clean

Once an area is fully cleaned, it should feel meaningfully different — not just technically complete.

Additional assets have been introduced for cleaned zones to better sell that transformation. This helps reinforce progress and gives the player a clearer visual payoff for their effort.

If the player reaches 100% and doesn’t *feel* it, something’s wrong.

---

## Small Quality-of-Life Improvements

A couple of small but practical additions made it into this build as well:

- An **Options** button has been added that opens the directory containing the `settings.ini` file directly.
- A new setting allows the **intro cutscene to be skipped**, for players who just want to get straight into the game.

These aren’t flashy changes, but they respect the player’s time, which is always worth doing.

---

## Closing Thoughts

This update was about tightening the experience rather than expanding it. It’s the kind of work that’s easy to postpone indefinitely, but the longer you leave it, the more it compounds.

The result is a world that feels more grounded, more readable, and more cohesive — without changing what the game fundamentally is.

If you’d like to see the full changelog or try the build yourself, you can find it on itch.io: [When The Mud Came - Itch.io](https://brazenbraden.itch.io/when-the-mud-came)

As always, feedback is welcome — especially on how the world *feels* after these changes.
