After more than ten years working as a Ruby on Rails software developer, I have officially launched [**Red Oryx**](https://redoryx.co.uk){:target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"}. Although my professional work in Ruby and Rails spans more than a decade, my interest in software goes back much further, to childhood and the days of QBasic.

Over the years I have worked across different organisations, systems, and codebases, but one thing has become very clear to me: the work I enjoy most is taking software that has become messy, brittle, or difficult to change and making it cleaner, safer, and easier to work with.

That has become a real theme in my career. I enjoy refactoring. I enjoy bringing structure to things that have drifted into chaos. I enjoy looking at a codebase that has become awkward to live in and steadily turning it into something more maintainable and more understandable.

## Why now?

For most of my career, I worked as a permanent employee, and there is nothing wrong with that. In many ways, it is the simpler path. You do the work, and a lot of the surrounding admin and responsibility is handled for you.

But there comes a point where you want to build something under your own name, on your own terms, and with a clearer connection between the work you do and the value you create.

That is where I am now.

Red Oryx is my move towards doing that properly. It is the identity I want to build around the kind of engineering work I care about most.

## What Red Oryx is about

Red Oryx is focused on practical Ruby and Ruby on Rails consulting.

That mostly means helping businesses improve and modernise systems that have become fragile, difficult to maintain, or risky to change. In some cases that means refactoring legacy code. In others it means improving delivery workflows, strengthening test coverage, cleaning up technical debt, or helping a team make safer decisions about how their software evolves.

The focus is not on buzzwords or inflated promises. It is on doing useful work that leaves a codebase and a team in a stronger position than before.

I am also building Red Oryx around a small trusted network of developers I have worked with for years. People I know well, trust professionally, and would be confident bringing into serious client work when needed.

## Why the name?

The name **Red Oryx** is personal, and closely tied to the identity I wanted the business to have.

I am Namibian, and the oryx is a significant animal in Namibia. It appears on the Namibian coat of arms and carries a strong sense of resilience, strength, and identity. It is a striking and distinctive animal, and one that felt like a natural fit for a business I wanted to build around those same qualities.

The **red** part of the name adds another layer. Red is a strong colour. It suggests passion, energy, and confidence, but it also connects naturally to **Ruby**, which has been at the core of my career for years. So the name reflects both my background and the technical world I work in.

Put together, **Red Oryx** felt strong, distinctive, and meaningful. It connects my work to my roots, my values, and the craft I have spent years building around.

## A new chapter

Starting a company is not the easiest path, but it is the right one for me.

Red Oryx is the next step in a career that has already given me a lot of experience across software development, legacy systems, refactoring, and the realities of maintaining real-world applications over time.

Now it is time to take that experience and apply it more directly under a banner of my own.

**Red Oryx is officially underway.**
